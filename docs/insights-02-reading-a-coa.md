# How to read a Certificate of Analysis in under a minute

A Certificate of Analysis is the one document that tells you whether a peptide supplier actually knows what is in the pen they just sold you. Most buyers either never see one, or get a generic PDF that could belong to anyone.

Here is how to read a real CoA in 60 seconds, and the four red flags to run from.

## The three things to check first

Before you read anything else, look at three numbers. Together they tell you ninety percent of what you need to know.

**1. Purity by HPLC.** Find the chromatogram. Look for the main peak. Next to it, there should be a percentage. That number is the area under the main peak as a percent of all peaks in the sample. If the main peak is, say, 99.13% of the total area, the compound is 99.13% pure by HPLC.

A credible supplier is showing you ≥97% for a release specification and ≥98% for a typical lot result. If you cannot find a percentage on the chromatogram, the CoA is not useful.

**2. Molecular weight confirmation by mass spectrometry.** Find the MS section. It will show a theoretical mass (what the molecule *should* weigh) and an observed mass (what the lab measured). If those two numbers match within a small tolerance, the compound you are holding is the one on the label.

A sequence check is often included next to the MS data. You do not need to read it yourself. You just need to see that it is there.

**3. The batch and date.** A real CoA is tied to a specific batch number, a production date, and an expiry. If you cannot find a batch reference on the CoA you were sent, the CoA is generic, and generic CoAs are marketing.

## A worked example

Here is what a healthy CoA line looks like in practice.

> **Product:** Epitalon
> **Lot:** 25AUG03EPI
> **Purity (HPLC):** main peak at 6.477 min, 99.13% area
> **MS:** theoretical 390.35, observed 390.36
> **Release spec:** ≥97% / Lot result: >98%

Four numbers. Together they tell you the compound is what the label says it is, and that the specific batch you received hits a purity level you can verify.

## The four red flags

You will see all of these in the wild. If you see any of them, ask for a better document or walk.

**Red flag 1: A CoA that is not lot-specific.** A generic PDF that says "typical purity >97%" with no batch number, no date, and no chromatogram is a brochure, not a certificate.

**Red flag 2: No mass spectrometry.** HPLC alone tells you the main peak is ninety-something percent of *something*. Mass spec tells you *what* that something is. Without MS, you are trusting the label.

**Red flag 3: Images instead of data.** A CoA should have numeric tables. If it is a rendered marketing graphic with large percentages and no supporting data, it was made by marketing, not QA.

**Red flag 4: A CoA that refuses to name the manufacturer.** A credible CoA is traceable to a named facility. "Hybrid synthesis, EU GMP certified" without a facility name or address is a dodge.

## What a good CoA does not need

A few things buyers worry about that are not actually required for a credible document.

- **FDA approval.** Research peptides are RUO. A CoA does not need an FDA sign-off to be valid for research use.
- **Third-party verification.** A manufacturer's own CoA is standard. Third-party retests are a bonus, not a requirement. If the manufacturer has its own HPLC and MS, the document is legitimate.
- **Fancy design.** A good CoA looks boring. Tables, numbers, signatures, footers. Flashy CoAs are often compensation.

## What to do with the CoA once you have it

Save it. Archive it with the batch number and date. If anything ever goes wrong with a compound, the CoA is your starting point for resolving it. If your supplier cannot give you one at all, you do not have a supplier. You have a dealer.
