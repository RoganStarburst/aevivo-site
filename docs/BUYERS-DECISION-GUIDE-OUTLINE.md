## Page 1 - Why this guide exists

Opening hook: peptides have become one of the most talked-about categories in longevity, and one of the least regulated. Most buyers have no way to verify what they are actually receiving.

This guide gives you a short, practical framework for separating a serious supplier from a surface one. It is written to be useful regardless of who you buy from.

**Key line:** If this guide helps you ask one more question before your next order, it has done its job.

---

## Page 2 - The purity question

What "purity" actually means (HPLC verification, area-under-the-curve at a given retention time).

What a Certificate of Analysis is, what it should contain (HPLC chromatogram, mass spectrometry confirmation of molecular weight, appearance, solubility, storage conditions).

Why ≥97% is a real threshold and why claims below it or without documentation should be treated as unverified.

**Red flag:** A supplier who cannot show you a CoA. Or shows you one that looks generic, stock, or not linked to their own production.

**Pro tip box:** How to read the chromatogram in 30 seconds - the main peak should represent >97% of the total area, side peaks small and well-separated.

---

## Page 3 - Origin and provenance

Where the peptide was actually synthesized matters. Options in the market today:
- European GMP facilities (highest oversight, most expensive)
- US research labs (variable)
- Chinese-sourced "research-grade" (cheapest, least verifiable)

What GMP means. Why "GMP certified" is worth more than "GMP inspired."

**Red flag:** Vague "research-grade" or "pharmaceutical-grade" language with no named manufacturer, no named country, no facility certifications.

---

## Page 4 - Delivery format

Lyophilized powder vs. pre-filled liquid. Reconstitution risks. Vial vs. pen cartridge.

Why pen format eliminates variability: sealed cartridge, inert atmosphere, no repeated septum puncture, dial-based dosing.

**Red flag:** No information about format, storage, or stability. "Just mix with bacteriostatic water" is an incomplete instruction.

---

## Page 5 - Storage and shipping

Cold chain matters for some compounds, is less critical for others. The supplier should tell you which applies.

What temperature-controlled packaging looks like. How you can verify the product arrived in spec (appearance, documentation, temperature indicator strip if used).

**Red flag:** Peptides shipped with no temperature control, no storage instructions, and no guidance on what to check on arrival.

## Page 6 - Pricing signals

A rough price range by compound class (BPC-157, GLP-1 class, bioregulators, growth hormone secretagogues) - a reference table, not a buying guide.

Why prices that are substantially below market are usually a quality signal, not a deal.

**Red flag:** Prices that look too good, paired with no CoA, no named manufacturer, no support channel.

## Page 7 - Who you're buying from

Company transparency markers:
- Named founders (not an anonymous brand)
- Real registered address
- Responsive customer support (email, phone, or both)
- Clear batch production cycles
- Refund or quality policy in writing

**Red flag:** An LLC with no website team page, no named humans, and no clear way to reach anyone if something goes wrong.

---

## Page 8 - The Buyer's Checklist

A single-page tear-out summary. 10 yes/no questions. Aimed at being printed or saved as a reference.

1. Is the supplier's country of synthesis named?
2. Is a GMP or equivalent certification documented?
3. Is a Certificate of Analysis available for the specific compound?
4. Does the CoA show ≥97% purity by HPLC?
5. Does the CoA include mass spectrometry confirmation?
6. Is the storage protocol specified?
7. Is cold-chain shipping used if required?
8. Is pricing within a reasonable market range?
9. Are the company founders or leadership identifiable?
10. Is there a direct support channel (not just a form)?

If more than two of these are "no," treat the supplier as unverified.

## Page 9 - Common myths

Short-form debunks of claims that circulate in online peptide communities.

Example candidates:
- "Purity doesn't matter, the compound is the same either way" (false, impurities include truncated peptides and counter-ions that can alter biological activity in research models)
- "Color indicates quality" (partially true but not sufficient)
- "All suppliers buy from the same three factories" (false, there are real differences)
