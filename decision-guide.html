Introduction

Most buyers have no way to verify what they're receiving.

Peptides are everywhere. Rigor is rare. This guide gives you a short, practical framework for telling the difference.

The peptide market has exploded in the last five years. Along with it, a quiet secondary market of vague suppliers, generic Certificates of Analysis, and prices that look too good to be true. Most buyers have no trained way to tell a serious supplier from a surface one.

What follows is seven things that matter, one short section each, plus a one-page checklist you can print. Read it in under fifteen minutes. Keep it on hand.

01 · The Purity Question

Purity is the first test. Most suppliers fail it.

When a peptide is synthesised, the process produces a mixture: the target peptide, incomplete chains, deletion sequences, and by-products. "Purity" is the proportion of the final product that is the target peptide. It is measured by High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC).

Simplified HPLC Chromatogram

What to look for

  • A chromatogram that shows the main peak and the smaller peaks around it.
  • A percentage next to the main peak, usually labelled as % area.
  • A release specification (the minimum the supplier commits to) and a lot result (actual measurement for this batch).
Red flag. Generic PDF not linked to a batch, no chromatogram at all, or a percentage with no supporting image.

02 · Identity

Verify the peptide is the peptide on the label.

HPLC tells you the main peak is some percent of the total. It does not tell you what that peak is. Credible suppliers run mass spectrometry (MS) to confirm it.

Mass spec reports two numbers: the theoretical mass (what the target molecule should weigh, calculable from its formula) and the observed mass (what was measured in the sample). If the two match within a small tolerance, the peptide is what the label says.

Example: Epitalon

Theoretical mass: 390.35 Da  ·  Observed mass: 390.36 Da

Difference of 0.01 Da is well within instrument tolerance. Peptide identity confirmed.

What to look for

  • An MS spectrum image or table showing theoretical vs. observed mass.
  • The peptide sequence spelled out on the CoA (for example, Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly for Epitalon).
  • For recombinant peptides, a separate N-terminal or DNA sequencing check of the expression strain.
Red flag. Purity data shown but no identity verification. The peptide could be anything with the right molecular size distribution.

03 · Origin & Provenance

Country of synthesis is part of the product.

The peptide market has three broad origin classes. They produce three very different products, at three very different price points.

The resale trick. Many Western European and US "research-grade" suppliers actually buy their raw material from bulk manufacturers in China, Pakistan, or India, repackage it, and market it as EU-synthesised. Some of it is fine. Some of it is not. Without a named facility, there is no way to know which.
Red flag. "EU research-grade" or "GMP-inspired" with no named manufacturing facility, country, or certification documents.

04 · Delivery Format

How the peptide arrives changes what you received.

Research peptides reach buyers in three formats. They are not interchangeable. The format determines stability, contamination risk, dosing reproducibility, and price.

Cheapest

Lyophilised vial

  • Freeze-dried powder
  • Buyer reconstitutes with water
  • Introduces measurement errors
  • Multi-puncture contamination risk
Mid-Tier

Pre-filled vial

  • Ready to use
  • Still requires septum puncture
  • Each use exposes peptide to air
  • Dose measurement by syringe
Premium

Sealed pen cartridge

  • Closed-system, no puncture
  • Dial-based precision dosing
  • Inert atmosphere inside
  • Reproducible across series
Red flag. The supplier does not specify the delivery format on the listing, gives no storage protocol, or sells only lyophilised material with no reconstitution guidance.

The Price Story

Pens and vials are not the same product.

Comparing a vial to a pen is like comparing flour to bread. Both contain the same ingredient. One of them is ready to use.

This is the most common mistake buyers make: assuming a pen and a vial of the same peptide should cost roughly the same. They should not. They are categorically different products, at different points in the manufacturing chain.

What you get in a vial

The peptide, dissolved or lyophilised, in a glass container with a rubber septum. The buyer provides: the needle, the syringe, the reconstitution water (if lyophilised), the sterile technique, the dose measurement, and the risk of every one of those steps going wrong.

What you get in a pen

The peptide, pre-dissolved in a stability buffer, sealed inside a medical-grade cartridge under inert atmosphere, loaded into a dial-dosing device, tested for sterility and endotoxin levels, and formulated for refrigerated shelf life. No reconstitution, no puncture, no dose guesswork.

Why pens cost three to five times more. You are not paying three times as much for peptide. You are paying for the dissolution, the buffer, the stabilisers, the sterile fill-and-finish, the inert-gas seal, the dial mechanism, the sterility testing, and the quality release process. The peptide is the cheapest ingredient on that list.

How to think about pricing across formats

Compare pen to pen. Compare vial to vial. Compare lyophilised to lyophilised. If a supplier quotes a vial price next to a competitor's pen price and claims their offer is better value, they are either misinformed or deliberately obscuring the comparison.

At a glance

A vial is raw material. A pen is a delivered system.

Across the market, pens typically run 3-5x the equivalent vial price for the same peptide, and the gap is real manufacturing cost, not markup.

05 · Storage & Shipping

Most peptides that arrive broken were broken in transit.

Peptides are proteins. Many of them degrade above certain temperatures. A peptide that ships in an ambient box across a warm climate in July may arrive intact in structure but with reduced potency that no visible inspection will reveal.

Typical storage window

+2°C to +8°C refrigerated. Most liquid peptide formulations.

Do not freeze. Freeze-thaw cycles denature many peptides.

Away from direct light and heat. UV and high temperature accelerate degradation.

What to look for

  • A named storage temperature range on the CoA and the product listing.
  • Cold-chain shipping (gel packs, insulated box) when destination climate or peptide stability requires it.
  • Written instructions for what the buyer should check on arrival: appearance, temperature indicator strip, documentation match.
  • A 24-72 hour inspection window to report damage or documentation issues.
Red flag. No cold-chain option offered, no inspection window, no replacement policy if the shipment arrives out of spec.

06 · Pricing Signals

Prices that look too good usually are.

Each step in a legitimate peptide supply chain has a real cost. GMP synthesis, HPLC purification, analytical QC, sealed-pen formulation, cold-chain shipping. A supplier charging significantly below market is either absorbing losses they cannot sustain, or skipping steps.

We are not publishing a market price table here because prices shift quarterly. The better rule: get quotes from at least three suppliers for the same peptide at the same spec before deciding. If one is 40% below the other two, the 40% is a signal, not a deal.

What to look for

  • Prices that fall within a reasonable spread of the market.
  • A clear specification alongside the price (concentration, volume, format, purity spec).
  • Clarity about what is and is not included (shipping, customs, taxes).
  • Batch-cycle pricing: serious suppliers that produce in waves often quote per-cycle rather than flat retail.
Red flag. Prices far below market, paired with no CoA, no named manufacturer, no defined delivery format, no return policy.

07 · Who You Are Buying From

A named human beats an anonymous LLC every time.

If something goes wrong with a peptide, a shipment, or a documentation gap, you want a person to talk to. This is the single most underweighted factor in peptide purchasing, and the one most likely to matter when you need it.

What to look for

  • Named founders or leadership on the supplier's site.
  • A registered business entity with a real address and a country of registration.
  • A direct support channel, not just a generic contact form.
  • A written refund and quality policy.
  • Evidence of operational longevity and a reputation to protect.
Red flag. Anonymous brand, no team page, only a generic contact form, no registered address, no written policy.

The Checklist

Ten questions for any peptide supplier.

Run this before every order. If more than two answers are "no," treat the supplier as unverified.

  1. Is the country of synthesis named on the CoA or product page?
  2. Is a GMP or equivalent facility certification documented?
  3. Is a Certificate of Analysis available for the specific peptide?
  4. Does the CoA show ≥97% purity by HPLC?
  5. Does the CoA include mass spectrometry confirmation of identity?
  6. Is the delivery format (lyophilised, pre-filled vial, pen) specified?
  7. Is a storage protocol provided, with cold-chain shipping where needed?
  8. Is the pricing within a reasonable market range for the spec?
  9. Is a human or named entity clearly responsible for the supply?
  10. Is there a direct support channel and a written quality policy?

Applied Example

What a credible CoA actually looks like.

To make the framework concrete: a simplified CoA summary line for a single peptide. Every item below is either numeric or traceable. Nothing is marketing language.

Product: Epitalon
Lot: 25AUG03EPI
Manufacturer: EU GMP-certified facility (Lithuania)
Purity (HPLC): main peak at 6.477 min, 99.13% area
Identity (MS): theoretical 390.35 Da, observed 390.36 Da
Release spec: ≥97% / Lot result: >98%
Sterility: complies (Eur. Pharm. inoculation method)
Endotoxin level: < 0.20 EU/mg (limit < 10)
Storage: +2°C to +8°C, do not freeze
Expiry: 08/2027

Ten data points. Together they answer whether the peptide is what the label says, whether the specific batch meets a purity threshold you can verify, and whether the manufacturer is traceable and accountable.

When a CoA gives you this much information, you have something to work with. When it does not, you are trusting a brochure.
